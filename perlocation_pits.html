Perlocation Pits

Percolation pits are shallow excavations filled with porous materials, such as gravel, sand, or broken bricks, designed to allow rainwater or treated wastewater to slowly infiltrate the soil and recharge groundwater, thereby reducing stormwater runoff, improving water quality, and increasing water availability for non-potable purposes.

These pits are cost-effective, easy to construct, and highly adaptable to various soil and climatic conditions, making them a widely used method in urban and rural areas alike. Additionally, they help mitigate the risks of urban flooding and contribute to sustainable water management practices by enhancing the natural replenishment of groundwater reserves.

How Perlocation pits work

Percolation pits collect rainwater or treated wastewater from surfaces, allowing it to flow into the pit. The pit is filled with porous materials like gravel, sand, or broken bricks, which facilitate infiltration.

As the water flows into the pit, it infiltrates the soil through the porous materials. This process is called percolation.

The infiltrated water then moves downward through the soil layers, eventually recharging the groundwater.

Through this process, percolation pits help reduce stormwater runoff, improve water quality, and increase water availability for non-potable purposes.

Benefits of Perlocation pits

1. Recharging Groundwater:
Percolation pits help replenish groundwater, maintaining healthy aquifers and preventing land subsidence.
2. Reducing Stormwater Runoff:
By allowing rainwater to infiltrate the soil, percolation pits decrease stormwater runoff, alleviating pressure on urban drainage systems.
3. Improving Water Quality
Percolation pits filter out sediments and contaminants from the water, enhancing water quality.
4. Increasing Water Availability:
Percolation pits provide a source of water for irrigation, toilet flushing, and other non-potable purposes.
